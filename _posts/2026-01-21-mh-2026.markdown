Mystery Hunt is a funny time of year. You explain to your friends why you're
traveling to Boston in the winter. You try to convey the scale of the event
you're attending, while knowing your friends don't quite understand. And although
it ostensibly takes place over one weekend, the prep work and wind-down makes it
really last for two weeks.

As part of that wind-down, I've realized I need to write this post quickly, otherwise
I'm not going to get any work done this week.

# Pre-Hunt

The time running up to Hunt was more stressful than usual...very briefly, I typically
hunt with teammate. This year, some people on teammate were interested in splitting
into a new team. This got messy, but I share the opinion that a split was never
going to be clean. I joined the split, and here we are.

The new team, christened Level 1 Spicy, was 33 people,
so I had little expectation of finishing Hunt. However, I did think it was
*possible*, since I knew how strong some of those people were. Teams like Unicode
Equivalence have finished Hunt before, and combined with rumors that Cardinality
was targeting a shorter hunt, I thought we had a shot.

This was somewhat tempered once we got into Hunt proper, and started hitting
hard puzzle walls. It certaintly wasn't the hardest Hunt I've done, but things
got pretty crazy (in a good way). When Cardinality stopped by our HQ Saturday afternoon saying
we had a shot at finishing, I did not believe them, and my hopes continued dropping
when the Coin hadn't been found by Sunday noon. In the end, we got there, thanks
to aggressive endgame hinting (which I'll discuss later).

# Friday

I started my Friday by getting breakfast at Verveine, but this was breakfast at 8am,
so it was long before any crowds come in. On the walk over, Ivan asked me if I
had a spare server to host a meme. I did not, but he eventually found one
to deploy [levelxspicy.cooking](levelxspicy.cooking). This site was connected to
a Discord bot in our server, where anyone could `/cook` or `/uncook`
to update how much the team was cooking or getting cooked.

We got to 10-250 early to make sure we got seats, but someone from Cardinality said a
seminar (?) was going on, and we wouldn't be allowed in until close to Hunt start.
Only as I write this do I realized that maybe this was an in-universe seminar
for the CRYPTIC conference. I did do the pre-Hunt, but it was so long ago that
I'd completely forgotten the theme.

I had high hopes for Cardinality's Mystery Hunt, because I know I'm in the same
generation of Internet culture, and I was not disappointed by the PuzzMon. It
was interesting to hunt on a team that fit in a single classroom, rather than
shuffling people between two rooms like we did before. It certainly made
coordination easier.

Several of us were curious what Cardinality meant by team coordination over limited
resources being important (the leading team meme was that we'd be playing Farmville).
This year, Hunt used a Research Points (RP) system. Small scavenger hunt style
tasks would give RP, which could be spent to unlock puzzles or rounds. In the early round (the Kingdom), RP was the only way to unlock puzzles, with a hard cap on the number
of open puzzles at once. Later, we would unlock Dimensions rounds, which had
more typical Hunt unlocks where new puzzles were driven by round progression.

The RP system was pretty complex, but I think all the pieces worked. I liked the
way it distributed scavenger hunt tasks over the entire weekend, rather than one
big concentrated chore.
The way it was phrased made it feel like we needed to be really aggressive
on earning RP, and we didn't realize how generous the RP was until midway through Friday,
when we saw we had way more RP than we'd need given the pace of Hunt unlocks. Still,
it's better for teams to be too rich rather than too poor.

I could see the RP system being especially good at giving newer solvers a thing
to do if they were intimidated by the puzzles, but for our team in particular,
it was very hard to justify sending a marginal person off on a long Research Task,
so we gradually shifted to only doing the tasks that seemed super fast and ignored
the rest. I think this is the same as a typical Scavenger hunt, where you aren't
going to do every entry in the list, but it felt different when the tasks
were dripfed one at a time, rather than given all at once.

Speaking of Research Tasks, there was also MonQuest, an interactive overworld
where you could find PuzzMon and solve small puzzles for RP. I tried doing this
for a bit, before realizing I was only discovering things that someone had already
discovered. I ended up doing zero MonQuest after the opening round, but I did
work on...

[**Jumping to Conclusions:**](https://puzzmon.world/puzzles/jumping_to_conclusions)
Man, I solved so few puzzles in Kingdom this Hunt. After Land of No Name unlocked,
I got sucked into Dimensions nonsense and barely left...Anyways, this puzzle was
a bunch of fun. I'm a big fan of trying to cheese puzzles and I think the
"intended" solve path of each is signposted well enough if you've done enough
prior puzzles. I wrote a script to bash Skyscrapers, then worked on a script to bash
the final extraction. Scanning the output, I saw OMI???D in the output, and
suggested a thematic answer that could be correct. I explained that I wasn't
sure we should submit it, given this letter pattern was one of hundreds of
possibilities, and then someone else submitted it and solved the puzzle.

[**Somebody:**](https://puzzmon.world/puzzles/somebody)

[**Bringing Up Baby Shark (Capstone):**](https://puzzmon.world/puzzles/bringing_up_baby_shark)
To be honest, I did not notice the descriptions were labeled "Herring description"
rather than "Red herring description". The text was just clearly puzzly enough
for it to override my "is this a red herring" detectors.

There's no better place to discuss it, so 

[**🍓🏔️🎮:**](https://puzzmon.world/puzzles/2871)
Solving Baby Shark unlocked Land of No Name, and that was the last time I did
anything meaningful in Kingdom. The Land of No Name was a gimmicked round of
26 puzzles. Each puzzle started with all letters redacted, and solving a puzzle
would un-redact a specific letter across the entire round. After you solved 2-3
of them, you could be confident about what letter each puzzle would unlock,
which turned the solve process into one big prioritization game of deciding when
you had enough info to solve a puzzle, and which puzzles would give the most
useful letters.

If it wasn't clear, this round was super cool, and by the time we finished it
there were other Dimensions round nonsense going on, so I ended up losing track
of Kingdom rounds and never really caught back up.
